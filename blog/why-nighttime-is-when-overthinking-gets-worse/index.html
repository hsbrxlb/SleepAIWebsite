Why it happens

During the day, tasks, movement, and other people interrupt thought loops. At night those buffers disappear, so the mind can keep returning to the same worries or what-ifs.

What sleep science suggests

The brain is also more vulnerable to looping when it is tired but still alert. That tired-alert mix makes it harder to stop reviewing, predicting, and replaying.

What to do tonight

Shorten the loop by getting thoughts out of your head, lowering stimulation, and moving attention into a slower, more repetitive cue like breath, story, or guided audio.
